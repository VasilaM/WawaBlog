We spent 24 hours in a Wawa and wrote it all down

In the fall of 1974, Wawa, a Philadelphia-based convenience market, first opened its doors at 140 University Place. For the last 50 years, Wawa, then open from 6 a.m. to midnight, but now on a 24-hour operation, has been Princeton students’ most reliable source of early-morning coffee, late-night quesadillas, and everything in between.

Wawa is housed adjacent to the Princeton train station that connects the University to the cities that dot the Northeast Corridor. Within the Princeton community, Wawa is a watering hole for facilities workers, administrators, and students alike.

In a July 1984 article titled “The other side of midnight,” ‘Prince’ writer Michael Alcamo ’87 wrote, “It seems that the sun never rises or sets on Wawa as it does with the rest of the world. It’s like another planet, insulated from space and time. When did it all begin? When will it end, if ever?” Wawa is the site that binds together generations of University students in their perennial quest for mac-and-cheese after a night on the Street, caffeine to fuel senior thesis-drafting all-nighters, and a brief respite from Princeton’s ceaseless grind. There are few things certain in life: death, taxes, and Wawa.

The ‘Prince’ staff sat in Wawa for 24 hours, observing the rotating cast of characters who enter and exit its doors, and the stories of those who work behind its counters. Reporters switched off hour by hour, each observing a part of the Wawa tapestry. While we found that the sun does indeed rise and set at Wawa, the ecosystem of students and workers who traverse its walls is certainly insulated from space and time.
